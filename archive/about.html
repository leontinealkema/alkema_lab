Funding

We gratefully acknowledge funding for family planning research by the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation (PI Alkema) and for data quality issues in sibling histories by NIH (PI: Helleringer). We are passionate about collaborating with substantive experts and making available improved estimation methods and resulting estimates to diverse international audiences. To that end, we collaborate with various United Nations groups and organizations in the areas of global health and family planning. The statistical methods that we have developed for child mortality, maternal mortality, and family planning indicators [preobably some refs in those] are in use by relevant UN agencies and WHO for monitoring. Child and maternal mortality methods [refs] have been used to produce estimates for the final assessment of UN Millennium Development Goals and are in use for monitoring progress toward the Sustainable Development Goals. Family planning estimation methods [refs] are being used for monitoring in the FP2020 initiative by Track20 using the web-based application FPET (see fpet.track20.org). Lastly, we collaborate with \textit{the Guttmacher Institute} (link) on the estimation of abortion incidence and pregnancy rates, with intention status.

Overview

We develop statistical models to assess trends and differentials in key indicators in reproductive, maternal and child health and demography, focusing on two main areas:

  • Family planning ~ contraceptive use and need, unintended pregnancies, abortions, fertility
  • Child and maternal mortality

Model findings include population-level estimates of demographic and population-health trends as well as the description of global patterns of change, the identification of populations with outlying levels or trends, and comprehensive assessments of data quality.

Details

Main methodological challenges in this area of research are: - \item How to best use diverse data types with varying levels of bias and/or random measurement errors? - \item How to capture data-driven trends in data-rich populations, while also producing reliable estimates for populations where data are limited, with appropriate levels of uncertainty? - \item How to identify populations with outlying levels or trends? - \item How to construct probabilistic projections that are unbiased and well-calibrated? Our research has addressed these methodological challenges for various indicators via the conceptualization, development, and validation of context-specific Bayesian models. Key components of these models are: - \item the parameterization of biases and measurement error variances to account for data quality issues. - \item the specification of models that capture systematic trends or relationships, - \item hierarchical submodels to allow for sharing of information across populations to inform the estimates and projections for data-sparse populations, and Model findings include population-level estimates of demographic and population-health trends as well as the description of global patterns of change, the identification of populations with outlying levels or trends, and comprehensive assessments of data quality. Additional methodological contributions to the field include guidance on model validation \ssp{}, population-specific target setting \target{}, as well as the reporting and interpretation of population estimates and associated uncertainty \mikkel{} \cdata{}. We also contributed to GATHER, a new set of consensus guidelines which have been adopted by leading journals.
